A disjointed ecosystem

  • Inconsistent UI fragmented patterns across products.
  • Duplicated effort teams rebuilding the same things.
  • High tech debt compounding cost and slowing delivery.

Supply, Advertiser/Affiliate, and B2B/Demand looked and behaved differently — despite being one Expedia Group.

Research & Discovery~2 min

Uncovering a systemic problem

This wasn’t a top-down request to “make a theme.” The inconsistency surfaced through hands-on investigation across the partner ecosystem — and the evidence pointed to a structural issue, not a cosmetic one.

  • UI audit compared headers, navigation, components, and patterns across 7+ partner portals.
  • Stakeholder interviews with product, design, and engineering teams across programs.
  • Pain points surfaced duplicated components, divergent patterns, slow delivery, and no shared brand expression.

Design Principles~1 min

The principles guiding the system

  • One ecosystem, many products a single system flexing across radically different partner experiences.
  • Consistency without uniformity shared foundations that still let each product feel like itself.
  • Brand expression should scale Low / Medium / High volume, tuned to context.
  • Adoption must be incremental teams migrate on their own roadmap, not all at once.
Jeff Alford · Senior Experience Designer06
Tradeoffs & Decisions~2 min

Why a shared theme — and what we ruled out

Individual product branding
Rejected — would deepen fragmentation and duplicate effort across every team.
Complete redesign
Rejected — too costly and disruptive; teams couldn’t pause roadmaps for a ground-up rebuild.
Component-only standardization
Rejected — aligns mechanics but carries no brand expression or salience system.
Corporate-only branding
Rejected — ignores the distinct needs of Supply, Affiliate, and B2B partner contexts.

The decision: a token-driven shared theme — adopted incrementally, expressed at Low / Medium / High volume. The accepted tradeoff: slower, negotiated adoption in exchange for a cohesive system every team could own.

Adoption Challenges~2 min

Organizational challenges

  • Established patterns product teams had entrenched ways of working.
  • Competing priorities each team balanced its own roadmap and goals.
  • Brand vs. usability expression requirements competed with task efficiency.
  • Varying maturity engineering teams sat at different levels of design-system readiness.

How we addressed them

  • Shared principles common foundations everyone could align to.
  • Token architecture one source of truth, adopted incrementally.
  • Education workshops and guidance to build fluency.
  • Pilot programs proved value on real surfaces before scaling. [placeholder — name pilots]
  • Documentation made the system self-serve for every team.

What I Learned~1 min

Lessons from leading a shared system

  • Adoption is more about influence than design the hardest work was alignment, not pixels.
  • Systems succeed when teams feel ownership shared authorship beats imposed standards.
  • Incremental migration beats forced standardization meeting teams where they are sustains momentum.
  • Governance matters as much as components how the system is maintained decides whether it lasts.
